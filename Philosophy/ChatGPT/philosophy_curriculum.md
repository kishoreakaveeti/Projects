# Philosophy Learning Curriculum
## A coherent beginner-to-advanced roadmap

This curriculum is designed to help you **start from zero**, build a strong conceptual foundation, and then move toward deeper and more specialized questions.  
It is organized around a single central idea:

> **Philosophy begins when we seriously ask what is real, what can be known, how we should live, and what kind of beings we are.**

Instead of treating philosophy as disconnected schools or random famous thinkers, this roadmap **stitches the major areas together** so each module naturally leads to the next.

---

## Big picture map

Philosophy can be seen as four great questions:

1. **What is there?**  
   This leads to **Metaphysics**.

2. **How do we know?**  
   This leads to **Epistemology**.

3. **How should we think?**  
   This leads to **Logic**.

4. **How should we live?**  
   This leads to **Ethics and Political Philosophy**.

From these, other branches emerge:

- **Philosophy of Mind** — What is consciousness, self, thought?
- **Philosophy of Language** — How do words connect to meaning and truth?
- **Philosophy of Science** — What makes science knowledge?
- **Aesthetics** — What is beauty, art, and taste?
- **Existentialism / Meaning of Life** — What gives life meaning in an uncertain world?
- **Comparative Philosophy** — Greek, Indian, Buddhist, Chinese, modern Western traditions in conversation

# Stage 1 — Orientation: What philosophy is and how to do it
### Goal
Learn what philosophy is, what kinds of questions it asks, and how different branches connect.

### Notebook
1. **Introduction to Philosophy: The Map of the Territory**

### Concepts
- What philosophy is
- Argument vs opinion
- Premises and conclusions
- Branches of philosophy
- Why philosophy still matters
- How philosophical questions connect to everyday life, science, AI, and personal meaning

### Outcome
You should be able to say:
- what philosophy studies
- the difference between a claim and an argument
- how metaphysics, epistemology, ethics, and logic fit together

### Goal
Build the toolset for reading and evaluating philosophical arguments.

### Notebooks
2. **Logic I: Arguments, Validity, Soundness**
3. **Logic II: Deduction, Induction, and Fallacies**
4. **Logic III: Symbolic Logic for Beginners** *(optional but valuable)*

### Concepts
- Arguments, validity, soundness
- Deductive and inductive reasoning
- Common fallacies
- Strong vs weak arguments
- Necessary and sufficient conditions

### Notebooks

### Notebooks

# Suggested pacing

## Slow and deep path
- **1 notebook per week**
- Total: about **7 months**

## Comfortable path
- **2 notebooks per week**
- Total: about **3.5 to 4 months**

## Immersive path
- **3 notebooks per week**
- Total: about **10 weeks**

For long-term retention, the best path is:
- read notebook
- write your own notes
- answer reflection questions
- discuss one major objection
- revisit after a week

---

# How each stage flows into the next

1. **Introduction** gives the map.  
2. **Logic** gives the tools.  
3. **Epistemology** asks whether knowledge is possible.  
4. **Metaphysics** asks what reality is like.  
5. **Ethics** asks how to act in that reality.  
6. **Political philosophy** scales ethics to society.  
7. **Philosophy of mind** asks what we ourselves are.  
8. **Philosophy of science** studies knowledge in its strongest modern form.  
9. **Philosophy of language** studies how meaning and truth are expressed.  
10. **Existentialism** brings philosophy back to lived human experience.  
11. **Comparative philosophy** broadens the entire landscape.  
12. **Integration** helps you form your own view.

### Logic
- Graham Priest — *Logic: A Very Short Introduction*

### Epistemology
- Robert Audi — *Epistemology: A Contemporary Introduction*

### Metaphysics
- Peter van Inwagen — *Metaphysics*

### Ethics
- James Rachels — *The Elements of Moral Philosophy*

### Philosophy of mind
- David Chalmers — *Philosophy of Mind: Classical and Contemporary Readings* (later)
- Thomas Nagel — “What Is It Like to Be a Bat?”

### Existentialism
- Sarah Bakewell — *At the Existentialist Café* (friendly entry point)

### Comparative philosophy
- An Introduction to Indian Philosophy / Buddhist philosophy readers
- Introductory surveys on Confucianism and Daoism
